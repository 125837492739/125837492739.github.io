How do macromolecules affect the production of human thumbs?

The thumb is not a normal digit. It is the last digit to form and is the most commonly affected when limb disruption happens. It is defined by the overlapping of the HOXA13, TBX5, GLI3R, and HOXD13 transcription factors, without other HOXD transcription factors. (I will explain this later.)

Because it is the last digit to form, the thumb has a greater risk of disruption, with over 1100 syndromes listed in the OMIM database. However, because of its greater risk of disruption, it has the potential to evolve quicker.

Disruptions that decrease the size of fingers typically affect the thumb first.

One macromolecule that affects the production of thumbs is GLI3. GLI3 refers to both the gene that encodes the protein and the protein itself, which is a transcription factor. A transcription factor is a protein that regulates the transcription of genes. There are two types of transcription factors: activators and repressors.

During transcription in eukaryotes, RNA polymerase needs basal (general) transcription factors to bind to the promoter to start transcription.

Activator transcription factors bind to the DNA to make it easier for the RNA polymerase to bind to the promoter. Repressors can block the basal transcription factors, preventing RNA polymerase from binding to the promoter, preventing it from performing transcription.

GLI3 can be both an activator and a repressor. The balance of GLI3 activator and GLI3 repressor is crucial for the development of the digits. The activator form of GLI3 is distributed across the developing hand by the Sonic Hedgehog Pathway (SHH), which is produced in the Zone of Polarizing Activity (ZPA), located in the posterior part of the limb bud (the pinky). Since the concentration of SHH is high near the pinky, it creates more GLI3 activator in that region, creating a gradient with more activator near the pinky and more repressor near the thumb. The high amount of GLI3 activator promotes the development of fingers. On the thumb side, where SHH signaling is weaker, there is a higher concentration of GLI3 repressor.

Proper development of the thumb requires this repression of digit formation. However, if SHH signaling is completely lost, there will be an excess of GLI3 repressor, causing digits not to form.

To illustrate all of this with a real example, scientists experimented with a chicken and a mouse. In the chicken, they reduced SHH signaling. This caused an excess of the GLI3 repressor, causing it to have no digits.

In the mouse, which had a dysfunctional GLI3 gene, it couldn't maintain the activator to repressor gradient, which led it to have 8 digits, with none of them being the thumb (since GLI3 repressor is needed for that).

What macromolecules allow the muscles around the thumb to move the thumb?

The muscles in the thumb are made up of muscle fibers, which are made of sarcomeres, which are made up of parallel thin actin and thick myosin filaments. When you want to move your thumb, a nerve impulse causes calcium ions to be released, which then bind to the actin. This binding causes the actin to move, exposing the myosin binding sites. Initially, each myosin head is attached to an ATP molecule, but it releases the phosphate and attaches to the actin. Then it releases the ADP, causing the actin to move again. Finally, ATP binds to the myosin heads, separating them from the actin and allowing the cycle to begin again.

This movement of the actin results in muscle contraction.
