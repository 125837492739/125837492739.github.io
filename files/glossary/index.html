Gene

A segment of DNA that encodes for proteins.

Protein

A chain of amino acids that have many functions. They can mostly be divided into three uses: movers, builders, and pumps. Some examples of proteins are ATP synthase, lactase, and DNA polymerase.

Enzyme

An enzyme is a kind of protein that helps in chemical reactions. They are very specific, often only working for one particular chemical reaction.

To help a chemical reaction, enzymes can bind to specific reactant molecules, called substrates. Common things an enzyme can do is to break down a substrate, or to combine multiple substrates into a larger molecule.

The part of the enzyme where the binding of substrates happens is called the active site.

Transcription

The process where the information in DNA is copied into RNA.

RNA Polymerase

An enzyme that is used during transcription.

Promoter

A part of DNA that tells the RNA polymerase where to start transcription.

DNA

A double helix structure that contains the information for how to make proteins. It is made up of nucleotides, which are made of deoxyribose, phosphate, and one of four nitrogenous bases: A, T, C, or G. The sequence of the 4 letters can encode for a protein.

RNA

A structure that is similar to DNA, but usually single-stranded and has ribose instead of deoxyribose, and with U instead of T.

ATP

A molecule that serves as the primary energy source in cells. It stands for Adenosine Triphosphate, which means that it is made of one adenosine molecule and three phosphates. ATP is commonly called the "energy currency" of the cell, since energy is released when breaking the bond between one of the phosphates and the rest of the molecule.

ADP

A molecule that stands for Adenosine Diphosphate. It is the result after breaking the bond of the phosphate in the ATP. It is similar to a discharged battery, in that it can be "recharged" by adding another phosphate to it, converting it back to an ATP molecule.

Concentration

The amount of a substance divided by a larger volume. For example, with sugar in water, the concentration of sugar is the amount of sugar divided by the total volume of the sugar and the water.

Gradient

A difference in the concentration between two regions.

Diffusion

The movement of molecules from an area of high concentration to an area of low concentration without external energy.

This works by a simple probability problem. If there are 20 people in a room, and 0 people in another room, and people start to walk around randomly, then it is likely that both rooms will be filled.

Therefore, it is not actually individual molecules moving from one region to another, but rather the net movement of molecules.

Channels

Proteins in the cell membrane that allow the passage of molecules. They can be gated, meaning that they can open or close.

The movement of molecules through channels is powered by diffusion, with no additional energy needed to move molecules from regions with high concentration to regions with low concentration.

Eukaryotes

An organism with a nucleus.

Prokaryotes

An organism without a nucleus.

Evolution

The process where living organisms change over time.

Variation

Differences in some trait of individuals in a population.

Heritability

The ability for a trait to be passed down to the next generation.

Selective Pressure

External factors that affect an organism's survival.

Reproductive Success

The ability of an organism to pass reproduce.

Unequal Reproductive Success

The difference of reproductive success between individuals in a population.

Change Over Generations

The change of traits across multiple generations.

Natural Selection

The process that drives evolution, in which organisms having advantageous traits are more likely to reproduce, passing that trait onto their offspring.

In order for natural selection to happen, there needs to be variation of traits in a population, which need to be heritable.

A selective pressure then kills many individuals, leaving the ones with the traits best fit to survive that selective pressure. That means that certain traits will have unequal reproductive success, meaning that there will be a change of the amount of individuals with that trait in the next generation.

Over time, the trait with the best reproductive success will become more common. Therefore, natural selection drives the evolution of a species over time.

Simple Mendelian Trait

A trait controlled by a single gene that follows simple rules of inheritance.

Allele

One of the versions of a gene.

Commonly, an individual inherits one allele from each parent. Typically when talking about a Mendelian trait, one of the alleles is dominant and one is recessive. There can also be incomplete and codominance, but I will not explain that in this glossary.

This means that if an individual has a dominant allele, they will have the trait that is defined by the dominant allele.

The recessive trait is only displayed when they have two of the recessive alleles.

When two parents produce offspring, the probability of the phenotype of the offspring depends on the genotypes of the parents. This can be calculated using a Punnett Square.

Heterozygote

An individual with one dominant allele and one recessive allele.

Homozygote

An individual with two of the same allele (dominant or recessive).

Genotype

The combination of alleles for a given gene.

Phenotype

The observable characteristics, determined by the genotype.
